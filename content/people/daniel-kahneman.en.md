## About Daniel Kahneman

Daniel Kahneman is an Israeli-American psychologist who is widely recognized as one of the most influential scientists of our time. He won the Nobel Prize in Economics in 2002 for his contributions to behavioral economics and the study of human judgment under uncertainty.

## Key Contributions

### Prospect Theory
Kahneman and his late collaborator Amos Tversky developed prospect theory, which explains how people make decisions involving risk. This theory revealed that people do not behave as rational economic actors but instead are subject to various cognitive biases.

### Availability Heuristic
His research identified how people judge the probability of events based on how easily examples come to mind—a phenomenon known as the availability heuristic.

### Anchoring Effect
Kahneman demonstrated that people are heavily influenced by irrelevant numbers (anchors) when making decisions, even when they know the numbers are arbitrary.

## Career

- **Princeton University**: Senior Scholar at Princeton's Center for Advanced Study
- **Hebrew University**: Previously held the Richard Salomon Chair of Cognitive Science
- **Author**: *Thinking, Fast and Slow* (2011) - A bestselling exploration of human judgment
- **Influence**: His work has revolutionized fields from economics to medicine to public policy

## Legacy

Kahneman's work has demonstrated that human decision-making is far more complex than traditional economic models suggest. By understanding cognitive biases, we can make better decisions both individually and collectively.

His insights continue to shape how businesses, governments, and individuals approach problem-solving and strategy.
